First Aid

Burns

Stop the Burning

Take person away from heat source. If he/she is on fire, smother fire with blanket, coat, or large amounts of water.

Do:
  1. Remove clothing contaminated with chemicals.
  2. Remove any jewelry if possible.
  3. Flush burns with large amounts of water for 15 minutes.
  4. Lightly cover the burn with a DRY sterile dressing.
Do Not:
  1. Apply any ointment or cream.
  2. Break blisters.
  3. Remove clothing that is sticking to skin.
Cover the Burn

Cover the area with a dry, clean dressing. The bandage should NOT put pressure on the burn.

Call 9-1-1 If:
  1. The burn causes breathing difficulty (especially if burned on mouth or nose).
  2. Burns are located on the head, neck, hands, feet, or genitals.
  3. The child has special health care considerations.
  4. Burns are covering more than one body part.
  5. Burns are the result of chemicals, electricity, or explosions.

Unconscious/Fainting

Unconscious Adult and Child (Older Than 1 Year Old)

Follow these steps:

  1. Send someone to call 9-1-1.
  2. If you suspect head or neck injury due to the person falling from a height or an accident, limit movement to the head and neck when opening the airway.
  3. Gently tap the person and shout his/her name to see if he/she awakens or responds.
  4. Position the person face-up and open the airway. Look for chest rise, listen for breath sounds, and feel for his/her breath on you face (no more than 10 seconds).
  5. If the person is breathing, roll him/her onto his/her side, and stay with him/her until help arrives. Check for breathing frequently.
  6. If the child is not breathing, or stops breathing, begin the steps for CPR.
Unconscious Infant (Less than 1 Year Old)

Follow these steps:

  1. Send someone to call 9-1-1.
  2. If you suspect head or neck injury due to the infant falling from a height, limit movement to the head and neck when opening the airway.
  3. Gently tap his/her feet and shout his/her name to see if he/she awakens or responds.
  4. Position the infant face-up and open the airway. Look for chest rise, listen for breath sounds and feel for his/her breath on your face (no more than 10 seconds).
  5. If the infant is breathing, ensure the airway remains open and stay with him/her until help arrives. Check for breathing frequently.
  6. If the infant is not breathing or stops breathing, begin the steps for CPR.

Choking

Conscious Choking Adult and Child

If the person can cough, speak, or breathe: Do not interfere. Send someone to call 9-1-1 and stay with the person until help arrives (if alone, bring the phone to you).

If the person cannot cough, speak, or breathe: Send someone to call 9-1-1.

Use abdominal thrusts following the steps below:

  1. Kneel or stand firmly behind him/her and wrap your arms around him/her so that your hands are in front.
  2. Make a fist with one hand and place your other thumb inside the fist.
  3. Put the thumb side of tyour fist just above the belly button.
  4. With your other hand, grasp the fist and give quick upward thrusts into the belly until the object is forced out, the person can cough, speak, or breathe, or loses consciousness. If he loses consciousness, begin the steps for CPR
Conscious Choking Infant

If the infant can cough, breathe, or cry: Do not interfere. Send someone to call 9-1-1 and stay with the infant until help arrives (if alone, bring the phone to you).

If the infant cannot cough, breathe, or cry: Send someone to call 9-1-1 and use back slaps and chest thrusts to relieve the choking infant. Sit on a chair or kneel and follow these steps:

  1. Hold the infant face down on your forearm suporting the head and jaw with your hand. Rest your arm on your lap or thigh keeping the head lower than his/her diaper.
  2. Give up to 5 back slaps with the heel of your hand between the shoulder blades. If the object does not come out, turn the child over, keep the head down and remove the clotes from the chest area.
  3. Support the head, and give up to 5 chest thrusts using your middle and index fingers from you free hand on the breastbone.
  4. Alternate 5 back slaps and 5 chest thrusts until the object comes out, the infant can breathe, cough, or cry, or the infant loses consciousness. If he/she loses consciousness, begin the steps for CPR
Unconscious Choking Adult, Child, and Infant

Head Injuries

Call 9-1-1 if the person:
  1. Is unconscious or hard to wake up
  2. Is having seizures
  3. Has vomited more than 2 times
  4. Is dizzy, complains of blurred vision, or has trouble walking
  5. You are unable to control bleeding after 10 minutes of direct pressure
  6. Is unable to move arms or legs
  7. Has blood draining from nose or ears
  8. Complains of severe headache, or cries inconsolably (non-stop)
Head Injury

Do:

  1. Place an ice bag or cold pack on the injured area to stop swelling
  2. Closely watch the person to see if symptoms develop or worsen
  3. Call your doctor with questions
Scalp Injury

Do:

  1. Place sterile (clean) dressing on the wound to stop the bleeding
  2. Apply pressure to the wound
  3. Monitor for signs of infection such as fever, swelling, redness, or discharge

Do Not:

  1. Move the person if neck or back injury is suspected
  2. Give anything by mouth if the person is unconscious or semi-conscious

Seizures

Care of people experiencing seizures:

Seizures may cause the entire body or parts of the body to shake. Seizures occur for many reasons, especially in children and can be frightening for the parent or caregiver. If you suspect someone is having a seizure, follow these steps:

Do:

  1. Stay calm. Most swizures only last a few minutes.
  2. Send someone to call 9-1-1.
  3. Make sure the person is safe, remove objects from around him/her.
  4. Keep the person on the floor. Not on beds or couches where they may fall.
  5. Check for breathing during and after seizure activity.
  6. Note the type of movement and duration of seizure activity.
  7. When the seizure is over, lay the person on his/her side.
  8. If the person stops breathing take the steps for CPR.

Do Not:

  1. Place or force objects between the person's teeth or in the mouth.
  2. Hold the person, or try to stop the jerking movements.
  3. Give the person anything to eat or drink.
  4. Give the person a cold bath.

After a seizure the person may:

  1. Be confused, or not remember the seizure.
  2. Complain of a headache, or feeling dizzy.
  3. Experience loss of bladder or bowel control during the seizure and need to be cleaned/change of clothes.
  4. Feel sick to his stomach and vomit or have excess saliva (drool) following the seizure. Be sure to maintain the airway by rolling the person on his/her side. or supporting an infant in your arms.

Eye/Ear/Nose/Mouth/Dental Injury

Eyes

Foreign body/objects like dirt/sand/slivers can fall or be blown into the eye. Chemicals can splash into the eyes and even a stray eyelash can be painful. Our eyes make tears naturally to flush objects out, but eyes can become very irritated.

Do:

  1. Encourage the person to blink and use natural tears to flush the object out.
  2. Using warm water, flush the eye over the sink. If the eyes were splashed with chemicals, flush immediately for at least 20 minutes.
  3. If the object does not easilly wash out, cover both eyes with dry gauze and seek medical help.
  4. Apply an ice pack to bruises/redness around the eye following an injury.

Do Not:

  1. Rub or put pressure on the affected eye to dislodge foreign body.
  2. Squirt or spray water forcibly into the eye.
  3. Use your fingers to takek an object out of the eye.

Call 9-1-1 if:

  1. An object is impaled into the eye.
  2. Blood and/or severe pain in the eye or deformity of the bones around the eye following a strike to the face or fall - follow the steps for Head Injury.
Ears

Injuries to the ear can include inner ear, ear canal, and outer ear. Possible injuries include: ruptured eardrum, infection, cut/bruise/abrasion, burn, bite, or foreign body in the ear.

Do:

  1. Wash a cut or abrasion (gently) with warm soapy water for 5 minutes.
  2. Cover with sterile gauze or a bandage to keep it clean and dry.
  3. Follow the steps for Burn if one occurs.
  4. See a doctor if you suspect a person has an ear infection. Complaints of pain, fever, and itching/discomfort are common signs of infection.
  5. If a person is bitten, contact a doctor for guidance. Call police to report a stray or unleashed animal.

Do Not:

  1. Push items into the ear to retrieve objects from the ear canal or stop bleeding.
  2. Squirt or spray water forcibly into the ear.

Call 9-1-1 if:

  1. The ears are bleeding after a fall or strike to the child's head - follow the steps for Head Injury.
  2. An object is impaled into the ear (do not try to remove it).
  3. A bite (animal or human) is severe, and involves the neck tissue or eye, and/or bleeding continues more than 10 minutes.
Nose

Injuries can occur on the outside or inside the nose. Some common conditions include: broken nose, bloody nose, foreign object in the nose, bruise/swelling, and abrasions/cuts.

Do:

  1. To stop external bleeding, apply 10 minutes of pressure with a sterile gauze.
  2. Use warm soapy water to (gently) wash any cuts/scrapes and cover with a bandage only if it does not interfere with breathing.
  3. To stop a nosebleed, have the person sit or stand up, and gently pinch the nose shut with a tissue or washcloth. Have him/her lean forward so that blood does not accumulate in the mouth or throat and provide a vessel if he needs to spit.
  4. To remove a foreign object, push the unaffected nostril shut and have him blow out the other nostril to see if the object will come out. Only remove the object with tweezers if it is visible.
  5. If you suspect someone's nose is broken (deformity or swelling may not require a trip to the hospital), call a doctor for guidance.
  6. Apply ice to a bruised or swollen nose, but only lightly, without pressure.

Do Not:

  1. Use tweezers, cotton swabs, or other tools to retrieve a foreign object you cannot see.
  2. Place ice or apply pressure to a nose you suspect is broken.

Call 9-1-1 if:

  1. The person has trouble breathing.
  2. The person is unconscious due to a fall or strike to the nose - follow the steps for Unconscious/Fainting until help arrives.
Mouth

Injuries of the mouth are usually not serious, but they can bleed a lot. Injuries of the mouth include: broken/chipped tooth, knocked-out tooth (avulsion), cuts to the tongue, lips or inside of cheeks, impaled object.

Dental

Do:

  1. For a chipped or broken tooth, gather the pieces and call the dental office.
  2. Place a completely knocked-out tooth in a container of milk or water and take it with that person to the Emergency Room. Often times, if the repair happens within an hour of the injury, the tooth can be saved. *Transporting a tooth with an awake, cooperatuve person: tooth may be held in mouth without swallowing.*
  3. Use sterile gauze to stop the bleeding from the socket.

Do Not:

  1. Handle he root of the tooth and don't scrub it or clean it.

Soft Tissue of the Mouth:

Bumps to the mouth can result in injuries to the lips and tongue. Usually applying ice wrapped in a paper towel or a cool washcloth will stop anybleeding and soothe the injured person. If the injury is severe, or bleeding does not stop after 10 minutes, the person should be evaluated by a health care provider. Lip burns from an electric cord should be evaluated urgently by a medical professional.

Call 9-1-1 if:

  1. An impaled object is in the person's mouth (do not try to remove it).
  2. The person is unconscious from the blow to the mouth or fall - follow the steps for Unconscious/Fainting.
  3. The person is having difficulty breathing.

Hot and Cold Weather Emergencies

Heat

Heat Cramps

  1. Symptoms: Painful muscle cramps, sweating
  2. First Aid: Move away from heat and give sips of cool water. Cramps can be massaged with gentle pressure.
  3. Doctor or 9-1-1: Doctor

Heat Exhaustion

  1. Symptoms: Heavy sweating, tired, pale, nausea, headache, rapid breathing
  2. First Aid: Move away from heat, give sips of cool water and remove clothing. Set person near a fan.
  3. Doctor or 9-1-1: Doctor, or 9-1-1 if the person's condition does not improve.

Heat Stroke

  1. Symptoms: Red dry skin, confusion, seizure, high temp. (103 degrees orally), vomiting, unconsciousness
  2. First Aid: Remove from heat and begin cooling step for heat exhaustion. Follow steps for Unconscious/Fainting if the person has passed out.
  3. Doctor or 9-1-1: 9-1-1

Do Not:

  1. Give the person a cold bath, or immerse him/her in ice.
  2. Let him/her return to the heat or active play for at least four hours.
  3. Leave the child unattended during cooling steps or recovery.
Cold

Frostnip

  1. Symptoms: An early warning of frostbite - affected skin becomes white and numb.
  2. First Aid: Bring the person inside and remove all wet clothing and immerse chilled body parts in warm (not hot) water until sensation returns.
  3. Doctor or 9-1-1: Doctor if the condition does not improve

Frostbite

  1. Symptoms: Affected skin becomes very cold and turns white or yellowish-gray and feels hard and swollen. Blisters may appear.
  2. First Aid: Bring the person inside. If feet are affected, carry him/her. Call 9-1-1 and remove wet clothing. Place clean cotton gauze between affected toes/fingers.
  3. Doctor or 9-1-1: 9-1-1

Hypothermia

  1. Symptoms: Cold, pale skin, confusion, shivering, loss of coordination, slurred speech, fatigue or lethargy (feeling sleepy).
  2. Bring the person inside and remove wet clothing. Call 9-1-1 if the child is unconscious and follow the steps for Unconscious/Fainting.
  3. Doctor or 9-1-1: 9-1-1

Do Not:

  1. Give the person a hot bath, or immerse affected skin in hot water.
  2. Rub or massage the skin.
  3. Use direct heat, like heating pads on the person's skin.
  4. Disturb any blisters.
  5. Administer medication unless directed by a physician.

Poisoning/Allergic Reactions/Bites

Poisoning

A poison is any harmful substance taken into or placed on the body. Poison can be swallowed, inhaled or injected. Some poisons have an immediate affect, or problems may be delayed for hours. Children can be poisoned by many things, possible sources include: prescription medication, household cleaning products, vitamins, lead, illegal drugs, carbon monoxide, plants, and alcohol.

If you suspect a poison emergency call the Poison Center immediately at 1-800-222-1222. Do not delay waiting for symptoms to appear. Call the poison center.

If the person is found unconscious or is experiencing immediate distress call 9-1-1 and follow the steps for Unconscious/Fainting or CPR if they are not breathing.

Other signs the person may have been poisoned include:

  1. Stains around the mouth, or strange smelling breath
  2. Lethargy (sleepiness)
  3. Vomiting
  4. Seizures
  5. Bright red skin, or rash
  6. Stomach pain
  7. Confusion
  8. Headache

Do:

  1. Have drug/poison containers available for medical personnel
  2. Move the person and yourself away from the source of the poison
  3. If conscious, place child in sitting position and monitor breathing
  4. The person may vomit, provide a vessel for him/her
  5. Stay with the person and monitor the airway
  6. If chemicals have contacted the skin, check with the poison center before flushing with water

Do Not:

  1. Give the person anything to eat or drink
  2. Induce vomiting or give Ipecac without direction from a medical professional
  3. Wait for symptoms to appear, call immediately if you suspect poisoning
Allergic Reactions

Allergic reactions can be mild or severe. Most common allergies begin in childhood and some allergies run in families. An allergic reaction is the body's way of responding to an "invader" by using the immune system.

Symptoms to look for:

  1. Swelling (near site of contact or bite)
  2. Itchy skin, red raised bump (hives), or blotchy skin
  3. Upset stomach, vomiting
  4. Runny nose, itchy watery eyes

Call 9-1-1 if the person:

  1. Has difficulty breathing - wheezing sound or gasping
  2. Has a swollen tongue, airway, and lips
  3. Is unconscious

If a person has been issued an EpiPen by a medical professional and is having an allergic reaction, use as directed and monitor the person's airway and breathing. If he/she becomes unconscious, follow the steps for Unconscious/Fainting or CPR if he/she stops breathing.

Bites

Bumps and Bleeding

Bruises and Bumps:

A bruise is a blue or black-and-blue mark under the skin surface. The skin is not broken, and it is tender to the touch.

  1. Crushed ice in a bag, or an ice pack wrapped in a towel may provide comfort.
  2. Administer pain reliever, such a acetaminophen (Tylenol) or ibuprofen (Motrin or Advil) as directed, or contact a healthcare provider.
  3. If the bruise occurs from a twisted joint, or is followed by swelling and and/or pain, apply ice, elecvate (if it is a limb), and contact a healthcare provider.
  4. Bruises that worsen, or bumps that swell too much, may signal a greater injury - contact a healthcare provider.
Bleeding

Bleeding can happen with a scrape, cut or any injury that opens the skin.

  1. Gently clean the wound with warm soapy water and remove loose gravel, dirt and grime from the injury.
  2. After drying the area, apply antibacterial ointment to kill germs then cover with a sterile dressing or Band-Aid. Watch for signs of infection like yellow discharge or fever. Call a healthcare provider with concerns.
  3. Sometimes pressure is needed to stop bleeding. With sterile gauze, squeeze and raise the extremity to slow blood flow to the area.
  4. Do not pull, or remove torn skin. After cleaning the wound, gently cover the skin with sterile dressing.

Do Not:

  1. Try to remove embedded objects - seek medical assistance
  2. Remove gauze used to stop bleeding, bandage in place

Call 9-1-1 if:

  1. If the bleeding is severe, or lasts longer than 5 minutes
  2. The person loses consciousness - see Unconscious/Fainting
  3. Complains of neck/back pain, or has difficulty walking or moving injured limbs
  4. Displays signs of shock (a condition that affects blood flow in the body), including chills, blue lips or fingernails, severe pain, dizziness, or rapid breathing.
Shock
  1. Send for medical help.
  2. Treat bleeding or stopped breathing (if you are trained) first.
  3. Don't move child if there is a serious head, neck, or back injury.
  4. Keep child lying down. An unconscious victim sould be laid on their side.
  5. Cover only enough to maintain body heat.
  6. Do not give them anything to eat or drink.
