Fire Safety

Home Fire Drills

  1. Know what your smoke alarm and carbon monoxide detector sound like.
  2. Pretend that there is a fire. Practice exiting the house.
  3. Use a whistle, horn, bell, or other signal to start you drill. Don't use a lit match, lighter, etc. to set off a smoke alarm because it could cause a real fire.
  4. Know how to roll out of bed onto the floor; crawl and your hands and knees below smoke where the air is clearer, and crawl backwards down stairs. If the room is smoky, cover your nose and mouth with a damp cloth the keep from breathing in smoke.
  5. Go straight to your Safe Meeting Place once safely outside.
  6. No one should ever go back inside for anything.
  7. Practice your drill during the day, at night, and in all types of weather.

Fire Facts

  1. Studies show that from the time a fire breaks out, a person has less than 4 minutes to escape the disabling effects of smoke and poisonous gases.
  2. Smoke can engulf a home in under 10 minutes.
  3. Smoke alarms sense smoke or other products of combustion before they can affects your senses.
  4. Smoke and deadly gases rise, so you will need to crawl low, below the smoke where the air is clearer to exit safely, all the way out, if necessary.

Know What To Do When Your Carbon Monoxide Detector Sounds

  1. If the CO detector sounds, leave even if you do not feel symptoms. Follow your Home Fire Escape Plan. Open doors and windows on your way out.
  2. Dial 9-1-1. Seek medical attention if you have symptoms. If the alarm sounds and there are no symptoms, dial a qualified appliance technician.
  3. If you suspect a leak or smell gas, call your local utility immediately. Never re-enter the home until you are told it's safe.
Symptoms of Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Symptoms can be like a common cold, the flu (without body ache or fever), a hangover or intoxication, food poisoning, depression, or sea sickness.

  1. Dizziness, fainting, bad headaches
  2. Ears ringing, blurred vision, forehead tightness
  3. Appearing drunk
  4. Nausea and vomiting
  5. Disorientation, loss of muscle control
  6. Fatigue, sleepiness
  7. Shortness of breath, unusual heart activity
  8. Sudden death

Fire Extinguishers

Fire extinguishers should only be used by adults (unless you're a younger person that was trained). Most homes use a "multi-purpose" Class A-B-C extinguisher.

Remember the PASS-word to use a fire extinguisher:

  1. P - pull the pin (or other motion) to unlock it.
  2. A - aim at the base of the fire, 6 to 10 feet away.
  3. S - squeeze the lever to discharge the agent.
  4. S - sweep spray left to right until the flames are out.

*Only fight small fires*
